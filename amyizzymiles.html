 Filling Boxes

X


In response to: Wright; Ellison

The three-dimensional, multi-media piece Filling Boxes by the collaborators Amy, Izzy, and Miles integrates light, image-texts, and objects in response to Richard Wright’s 1941 book 12 Million Black Voices. It consists of a plaster model of a plantation field and a cityscape made up of heavy, rough buildings decorated with broken pipes and arranged on top of a clock base. The two models, physically disconnected yet linked in their barrenness, stand as visual representations of the two worlds Wright presents in his history of America, the rural South and the urban North. The medium of plaster and the action of filling cardboard boxes with plaster points to the Great Migration linking these two landscapes. The viewer encounters these plaster structures through a projection of a moving, image- text composition that takes Wright’s words, now distorted and broken up, and suspends them in space as they are cast over the model. In this way, the textual becomes visual, the fragments of text resembling a subway-map, and becomes physically present in the model as light.

The use of text and space in Filling Boxes seeks to touch on a central theme in Wright’s book, the power of words, how they can confuse as much as they reveal, how they can be placed beyond one’s reach, and conversely, how they can be grasped in different spaces and forms, such as in the sermons given in a church or in music of a dancehall. Wright showcases how the history of America he presents in 12 Million Black Voices is also a story about a struggle to untangle and gain dominion over language. From the very beginning, Wright challenges the reader to reconsider what they think they know about black people, indicating that language has failed in the typecasts it creates: “the black maid, the black industrial worker, the black stevedore…” But Wright can’t fully explain this history with language alone, he must imbue his writing with images, paradoxes, and supernatural metaphors. Moments in the text that draw upon the magical and the inexplicable paradoxes of black life are emphasized in the projection, such as the line “the Lords of the Land had waved a magic wand and cast a spell upon us.” Finally, the way a textual education has intentionally been made inaccessible to black people, a point made both in Wright’s book and in Ralph Ellison’s essay on Wright titled, “Wright’s Blues” once again stresses the way language has the power to redefine space and relationships. The collaborators—in pushing the viewer to notice movement-migration, space-landscape, their own positioning in relation to the work, and text as image, as map—seek to explore how words can, as Wright notes, serve as a “gateway to a forbidden world,” and thus to new possibilities.

 Rapid Fire Response

X


In response to: Jafa; DeCarava and Hughes

In the third radical composition, the collaborators are responding to works Harlem is Nowhere, Arthur Jafa’s Apex and Roy DeCarava’s Sweet Flypaper of Life.

Through the performative piece, Rapid Fire Response by Hilditch, Torres and Wilson, there is an intention to create a piece that purposely adjusts the frequency to which you absorb information from each visual text. Through creating a reversal in the way the texts are interpreted, audiences are invited to recognize the visual frequency that dominates our understanding of both Jafa and DeCarava’s work.

Apex is a series of images collated into video, carried together with a loud continuous beat. Every beat is a new image, moving so fast you can hardly sit with one before within a second it moves to the next. These images range from highly commercial media adverts, to deeply disturbing portrayals of violence. From one to the next, the tempo creates an unease, the viewer is not allowed a moment to breathe, empathize, think, or consider. Audience members may become frozen, or overwhelmed by the onslaught of images that contrast and reduce the subject matter to a deep emotional reaction of discomfort.

Key to the composition is one word: Reversal. Reversal can be defined as a “change in the opposite direction, position or course of action. 1 ” When we consider Apex, a reversal of the work is to change its tempo. A reversal would be to force a performance of the work in which we have to sit with each image individually and absorb them. It would be a space where we allow an emotional or physical reaction to each image, in contrast to one singular reaction to the sum total of all the works in one. What would it be to freeze a frame? What would it be to leave a moment where the viewer must sit, work through the disturbance and react to it?

Sweet Flypaper of Life has a different frequency. It is a book, a portrayal of life which is separate from the distress of Apex. The reader is given room to sit with the fleeting moments of beauty and to contemplate, to get lost in this familial world. The book is beautifully bound, carefully curated. It moves the reader through interior and exterior space, through intimate and public moments, and through both light and darkness. The images deeply contrast to Apex, yet both of the visual texts are brought into this radical composition.

By taking the art from both Apex and Sweet Flypaper of Life Wilson, Hilditch and Torres create a book of their own. Each shot is given equal time for absorption. Each page is a new image. Each page is split and set up as a performance in which for 30 seconds, the artists take a pen and annotate the page. This is one’s thoughts and first impressions, reactions or feelings. The participant is forced to spend less time with DeCarava’s photographs, and a little too long with Jafa’s. It changes the frequency in which both works are supposed to be experienced, and with this a new visual experience is established. By the end of the activity, about a 15 minute exercise that feels much like an exam, a different sense of exhaustion and exhilaration begins. A reversal of the intention of both pieces is established.

This piece is both a mixture of the experience of the annotation, and the surveillance of watching those annotating. There are three elements at play- the doing, the watching, and the book object we are left with at the end. These three parts to the piece feel equally as significant in Torres, Hilditch and Wilson’s composition. It represents a sort of undoing of the visual frequency, in a way that helps the participant understand the works more deeply.

 Sittings

X


In response to: Jafa; Joseph

The 8-minute video Sittings by collaborators Amy, Izzy, and Miles aims to create an environment of sound and image that the viewer can inhabit as they dwell on the unstable imagery of distorted subjects at rest. The piece responds to Arthur Jafa’s development of a distinctly black filmmaking style founded on a technique he has termed “black visual intonation.” Rather than a focus on subject matter, Jafa’s style places precedence on how a film is shot and viewed, favoring irregular frame rates. In fact, the subjects in pieces such as Sharifa Walks (2015) and Crystal & Nick Siegrid (2017) are on paper unassuming—a couple on a walk and a close up of a person—but the way Jafa shoots his subjects transforms the mundane into an intimate, suspenseful experience. Sittings similarly plays with the mundane. Subjects are captured sitting, talking, and laughing, yet through distortion of the lens as well as post-production manipulations, the collaborators create a hazy confusion of abstracted figures and body parts. The result is a visual softness coupled with the simmering texture of the digital audio that promotes a conscious slowing down and compels the viewer to focus and become submerged in a slight movement of an arm or the fleeting outline of a profile. In dwelling on the mundane the viewer is compelled to question how small moments in the everyday can be transformative. The eeriness that emerges from the distortion and the superimposing of footage, creating ghost-like figures, also draws on the unsettling combinations found in a piece like Apex (2013) as well as in the two short films Until the Quiet Comes (2013) and Wildcat (2013) by Kahlil Joseph. The ambiguity in Joseph’s work over whether his subjects are in danger or in transcendant ecstasy (drowning/floating, fighting/playing, dying/living) creates the potential for a new space or for crossings into new dimensions. The collaborators in Sittings explore this creation of a new, transformative space by aiming to suspend the viewer in mundane moments made unstable and eerie.

 Before the World Was Bought

X


In response to: Akomfrah; Weems

Before the World Was Bought leads the viewer through a dystopian narrative of a young child whose creativity has been harnessed as the secret weapon for the Resistance, a revolutionary movement that seeks to subvert the oppressive regime of a hyper-digital world. The steady tone of the narrated piece, picking up on a similar pace found in Carrie Mae Weems’ People of a Darker Hue, creates a foreboding tone that builds up with the entrance of The Commander. The viewer is left to ponder: Is The Commander a villain or a hero? Is this a children’s story or a cautionary parable?

The power of creativity and imagination in the face of constraining circumstances lies at the center of the story. Riley, the protagonist, lives in a void that takes the form of a windowless room where all activity is regimented. The children operate outside the rhythm of the outside society with only old magazine images to link them to a world beyond the confines of the room. Despite many constraints, creativity exists within the four-walls. Riley in particular is able to transcend her circumstances and produce something new. She makes art with what would otherwise go unnoticed, the residue of a day’s work on her fingers, at a time outside the Commanders routine, when she is meant to be sleeping. She looks outside the horizon of what is possible. Creativity, which the Restinance believes lies in uninhibited children, is positioned as the rejuvenating force that can fight against the odds of an oppressive order, that can reveal a path towards a radical, new future. But is this a rosy ideal or is there something slightly amiss in this blind hope? The final scene in which the two characters walk out into the city is tellingly left ambiguous. What is the Commander able to see? How will the two proceed? Ultimately, these questions propel the viewer towards a contemplation over the possibilities and limitations of a creative practice focused on futurity.

The narrative behind Before the World Was Bought was written collaboratively by Izzy, Amy, and Miles who experimented with free association as a way to pursue a liberated mind and in doing so fight against the constraints and limited modes of thinking and producing art that result from medium, such as filmmaking, that carry pre-set conditions and precepts. Collaboration, therefore, served as a way to further transcend the limits of the individual mind through collective play, active listening, and an embracing of randomness, an exercise in collaborative narration that resembled an improvisation session. The science-fiction genre was chosen in response to John Akomfrah’s film The Last Angel of History (1995), which mixes documentary elements with scenes that lean towards science fiction. In it, the past, present and the future are conflated in various ways. Interviewees depict the estrangement of black existence as a science fiction story. Black and white images of the past are montaged into the otherwise color film, making historical figures and past events into fixed, “virtual references” to pull from Akomfrah’s essay “Digitopia and the Spectres of Diaspora.” Through sampling and music the past is collapsed and the future is aspired to through never-before heard sounds. This confusion over the past, present, and future both in imaged and real form inspired the collaborators to create a story where the viewer can ponder the relationship between these different temporalities.

 Out of Site

X


In response to: Hartman and Rowland

For their final radical composition, the collaborators Izzy, Amy, and Miles took the often cite-specific work of Cameron Rowland and the radical imagining of Saidiya Hartman’s historical practice as invitations to reconsider spaces and names on campus that may initally appear unassuming but that are charged with a history of slavery and white supremacy. By engaging in historical research and noticing the way it can constrain engagement with the past, the collaborations employ Hartman’s subjunctive voice and the sepia tone of a poorly-lit photograph to begin to look beyond the historical record by registering the eerie frequency these spaces still give off today.

The founding of East Pyne (then known as Pyne Library) in the late 1800s was intricately tied to the global slave trade. The New York lawyer Moses Taylor Pyne was the main donor and fundraiser behind the building’s construction. At its peak, the family company he inherited, Moses Taylor and Company, would transport and trade a fifth of all Cuban sugar exchanged with the U.S., a commodity dependent on a slave plantation system.1 By cultivating close relationships with Cuban planters, in part by offering financial support, the Taylor family was able to gain a steady hold on the market and acquire its large wealth.2 The introduction of a modern library served to propel Princeton from a “country college” to a leading university.3 With the university's ascension, the source of Pyne’s wealth was veiled and he was celebrated for his generosity and devotion to the institution.

Legal historian Lolita Buckner Inniss, writing on Princeton in the 1830s and 40s, concludes, “Blacks in Princeton were everywhere yet nowhere, frequently crossing town and gown lines and without an acknowledged place in the social array.”4 Perhaps this seen-unseen state explains the side entrances that are still present in East Pyne’s staircases. They prevented Black workers from trafficking the hallways which were left only for the white, male students and professors. It seems the architects, the construction workers, the instructors, someone along the line put effort and thought into constructing these secondary entries. In their afterlife, covered by brick and mortar and no longer functional, the side doors remain unassuming and neglected. At most, the subject of passing thoughts: a strange decorating decision perhaps or evidence of truncated plans. A closer observer notes the slight eerie change in the tone of bricks, in their texture...

East Pyne veils, whispers, echoes. A white man in a top-hat, in the business of cementing Princeton as a leading institution. A building connected through a history of slavery to Cuba, to the sweat and heavy breathing of claimed bodies, to the fertile soil, to the sugar cane stalk, to the smell of molasses, to the suffacating heat of the processing fecilities, to the train tracks built for easy access to the interior, to the port-towns and slave ships, to the pedllars, to the contracts...

1 Maeve Glass, “Moses Taylor Pyne and the Sugar Plantations of the Americas,” Princeton & Slavery, accessed April 11, 2022, https://slavery.princeton.edu/stories/moses-taylor-pyne.
2 Ibid.
3 Ibid.
4 Lolita Buckner Inniss, “Princeton Slavery, Princeton Freedom,” In The Princeton Fugitive Slave: The Trials of James Collins Johnson, 1st ed., 37–56 (Fordham University Press, 2019), 54, https://doi.org/10.2307/j.ctvp2n2t7.7.

Reflection

X

Instead of writing individual reflections on our experience as radical composers, our group thought assuming the first person plural voice for the final time would be most representative of our experience as a collaborative trio. Here is our attempt to distill our experience as a group into a paragraph:

Rather than collaborating on a unifying theme or theory that would then be interpreted by each member in their own way, as some other groups have done, our first instinct as a group was to produce a work where our individual modes of expression and contributions would fuse together into a single work and become difficult to separate. Our first radical composition, Loop, for example, showcases this dynamic in that the final work amounts to a set of scanned images that we can no longer tell apart. Is that my hand or yours? Who brought in that wire again? Why did we land on scanners again? What we do know is that the three of us were in a room with a scanner thinking about different images we could produce with this machine. Although we could individually decide what objects we could scan and whether they would move on the scanner or lay still, etc., the production itself was a three person job: someone holding down the scanner lid, another tapping on the scanner, another swishing a piece of rope. It was a collaborative experiment that required being receptive to each other's ideas and taking on and off different roles in order to support that idea coming to life. Initially shy and cautious of each other's responses to an out-there proposition (face on the scanner?), by the end of the night we became much more daring and comfortable with each other. On this night of experimenting we entered for the first time into a distinct rhythm. It was a rhythm that gave order to the creative chaos. A rhythm that signaled when repetition was required (place objects, scan, view, repeat) versus when a moment had come for someone to break the rhythm, for a new idea to emerge. It was a playful rhythm, that encouraged experimentation, that was driven by the logic of “let’s just see what happens” and by taking a risk. This rhythm that we recall developing during our first collaborative effort we are now convinced is unique to the synergy that emerges when the three of us are in a room working together. Of course, we can collaborate with others and enter another group dynamic, but it wouldn’t have our distinct rhythm, our district flavor. It wouldn’t give off the exact frequency the three of us found we could mutually register and operate at. It can only exist in its unique form when Izzy, Amy, and Miles are together in a room, talking something into existence, as was often our initial step when approaching a composition. The process itself of working under this collective voice called for creative interpersonal dynamics. How do you encourage pushing the group in one direction without cutting off other possibilities? How do you release yourself to the idea that the end product is not in the sight of any one person in the room? How do you go somewhere together when you don’t know where you’ll land? What to an individual might seem daunting and scary becomes an adventure and a challenge for a group. Part of working in a team has meant carrying each other, encouraging each other, and covering different bases for each other so that we might each have the freedom to delve more deeply into a particular avenue that we would otherwise not have enough energy or space to explore. This meant building off of eachother, filling holes for each other, and getting braver and more daring in our experiments. With this in mind, there is something bittersweet about our collaborative experiments coming to an end as it means the loss of a space of support and collective growth. We hope that sense of camaraderie and friendship that has blossomed between us will not end here.

—Izzy Hilditch, Amy Torres, Miles Wilson
