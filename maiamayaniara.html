In response to: Joseph, Young, and Anyanwu

I was really struck by Wildcat’s cinematography and how it reminded me of Decarva’s photographs in Sweet Flypaper of Life. There is a dim or dark quality to Wildcat that is similar to the way Teju Cole describes Decarava’s photographs as “playing with darkness.”

Teju Cole explains that cameras rarely made it easy to photograph Black skin and had a tendency to underexpose dark skin. I was really struck by the quote: “Instead of trying to brighten blackness, [Decarva] went against expectation and darkened it further. What is dark is neither blank nor empty. It is in fact full of wise light which, with patient seeing, can open out into glories.” To me this quote reminds me of how Professor Campt describes quietness not as the absence of sound, but fully expressive, and in the same way darkness is not the absence of light, but the presence of “wise light.”

When I was thinking of dimness and darkness and shadows in photography I was reminded of Professor Campt’s theory of the hum and whisper. In a similar way that the hum and whisper are in some ways opaque and there is a strain or work required to listen. Darkness also requires a similar work from the viewer. Additionally, the photographs in shadow are also a shield like the hum and whisper are in Opacity of Grief. I really saw this idea expressed further when Cole describes that when looking at Decarva’s work, which shares a mysterious quality that expands that notion of darkness “we might come to the understanding that they don’t have to give themselves up to us. They are allowed to stay in the shadows if they wish.” The shadows guard the subject offering space to be concealed.

Finally, in both Wildcat and Until the Quiet comes, there is a sense of stillness or calm. Like Arthur Jafa’s “Crystal and Nick Siegfried” and “Sharifa Walks,” the people in the frame are moving, but their movements are so slow that it might go unnoticed. Professor Campt describes this as a “quiet intensity.” I see a similar “quiet intensity” in Wildcat. I think the intensity comes in because it is not only the quietness that is registered sonically (we do not hear the voices or sounds of the rodeo), but also temporarily through the slowness of the film where the film becomes thoughtful photographs and images, which sound melds together.

The Cut

X



In response to: Jackson, Seeger, Seeger, and Seeger; Weems; and Nkiru

Our radical composition aims to answer how Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison and Carrie Mae Weems' A Darker Hue respond to institutionalized Black violence. In order to answer this question, we examined the use of repetition, cuts, and sound as a mode of survival, resistance, and refusal.

We created a multimedia slide exhibition using collage and music.

The process of musical translation of the visual frequencies emanating from the cuts in Work Songs and A Darker Hue illuminated several interesting observations and questions:

The first step in this process involved perceiving the listed works and becoming intimate with one’s intuitive reactions, one’s inner “bell” which resonates, in response to pacing, movement, repetition, variation, absurdity, color, timbre, and form. Maya found that they were especially struck by the hypnotic, trance-like pacing found in the repetitive form of the works which were interspersed with subtle cuts of play and virtuosity (ex. the twirl of an axe, the glimmer of a sparkling gem.)

What feels like a cut? How do we perceive slips and gaps in “normal” time, space, and perception?

Maya also found that this process was not one that could be forced with the mind—it needed time to seep into the subconscious in order to emerge naturally. There probably was not enough time for this to happen given the scope of the assignment, but Maya tried anyway to sit with the pieces to develop a sense of trust with them.

How do we unlearn prioritization of the mind/analytical framework in digesting our surroundings? How do moments of beauty make their way through one’s subconscious? How long does this take? How fast is the speed of trust?

After Maya recorded their musical translation, our group went through a process of pairing the visual presentation (already curated separately from the music) to certain cuts in the music, which we found through a group consensus. It was fascinating to see how we all had slightly different intuitions for the cuts in the music – Niara really enjoyed one cut that was subtle, like a hum or whisper. And yet there were certain moments where we all immediately agreed. A more universal cut.

How does the artist’s intention manifest in the listener’s ear? Is there some sense of the universal? How does truth move from one body to the next?

Our Radical Composition manifested in a performance involving audio and four slides of visual media interspersed with black slides. We first allowed the performance to run without verbal analysis and then later retraced our steps with personal explanation.

Slide 1: In Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison, we saw critical "cuts," which we put in conversation with People of a Darker Hue. The first cut is the falling tree. Not only does it cut the harmony and rhythm of the work song and labor, but it exposes the dangerous and life-threatening work the prisoners have to endure. We put the film still of the falling tree in dialogue with a still from Weems' video, which shows the visual history of anti-blackness and repeated Black death and violence. In doing so, we wanted to point out the particular sonic frequency of these two videos of Black violence.

We also wanted to enact our own cut of refusal and resistance using the mourning practice in Weems' video to respond to both acts of violence.

Our second cut was the narration in the Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison. We noticed how the narrator pushed feelings of happiness, safeness, and progress in problematic ways. We asked ourselves how we might get rid of the narrative or cut the narrative to show itʼs manipulative power. We pulled quotes from the narration and created poems to highlight the paradox between this video's visual and sonic frequencies.

Black Slide: The black slides in between each collage were our way to hold space for reading and reflection and slow the pace down so that the viewers could internalize the slides.

Slide 2: Our third cut is the context of the Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison. Here we illustrate the surveillance Black people endure. We cut to the white overseer to provide context for the violence that might not be so explicit or clouded by the prison's narrative as harmonious. Similar to slide 1, we put a still from Weems' video of police brutality in conversation with Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison and place a still of response to cut the two science of Black violence as an act of resistance. Finally, we created found poems like in Slide 1 using quotes from Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison to resist the erasure of this experience in the Texas prison.

Slide 3: We wanted to also look at forms of repetition in both Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison and Weems' People of a Darker Hue. Both the tilling of the land and the running on the treadmill are motions that repeat, but they also force us to question the systems that perpetuate the criminalization and death of Black people. Why is the man running? Is he running towards freedom? What would happen if he stopped? Does that mean he surrenders? Or is it a form of resistance and refusal? We created the third poem from a quote from Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison, "Prison is the only place left in this country where the work song survives, and its days are numbered." This quote suggests the reason for the existence of work songs, and it claims that when work in prisons is gone the songs will die with it. However, putting Weems' video in conversation with Afro-American Work Songs in a Texas Prison shows that prison work songs are not gone but continue existing in other forms because institutionalized black violence continues.

Slide 4: The first sequence of the Weems' video depicts an oscillation between blurry and clear visuals of the magnification of primarily Black and Brown faces. We might read these stills as calling attention to the camera as an instrument of surveillance, policing, and control. Two Black women make contact with the viewer, staring directly at the camera and making a hand motion where they encircle one of their eyes. At that very moment, there is a cut where the narrator says, "I donʼt give permission." Although we cannot be sure about what that hand motion means and what it means to them, we can read this as a refusal to be surveilled not only by us as viewers, but the eye contact resists our gaze and makes a larger statement against the camera acting as an instrument of surveillance.

Draft Theory

X



In response to: Wright; Ellison; DeCarava and Hughs; and Parks

Harlem is Nowhere includes Ellison's annotations and edits, which inspired us to develop our theory of the draft. The draft is something that is never fully articulated. The draft is a form of abstraction, which does not obscure but opens up a world to the intangible and indescribable. Not only do Ellison's annotations, scribbles, markings, and codes distort the pages, but as a reader, we have the privilege to see his process unravel. The draft is both an illustration of struggle and a form of liberation through its endless possibilities that await beyond it. It is a gesture to the future and meditation on the past. It exists in a liminal space between feeling and communicating.

The draft connects to the idea of James Snead's "cut" and "repetition. In the same way that the "cut" creates space for accidents, reflections, and revisions, the draft also invites a return that opens paths to new possibilities. The draft may also be an index but not like a trace of the referent. It can be equated to Roland Barthes' photographic paradox, which is said to be that of a "message without a code." The draft may also be a message without a code because it is personal and not completely transparent. The draft is a form of communication that lacks a direct relay through its opacity.

I attempted to visualize the draft by showing my notes and reflections on Harlem is Nowhere and Sweet Flypaper of Life. I created a map of my inner thoughts and connections, which may sometimes lack clarity and continuity. However, these annotations helped me become an active participant in grappling with these image-texts. Through the draft, I work to understand the entanglement of the repetition of Black suffering that Ellison describes.

The Offbeat

X



In response to: Jafa

I hope to visualize the "offbeat" through my photographs of architecture and absent spaces. Similar to our theory of the draft, I think that the offbeat allows room for experimentation and accidents. I consider these photographs similar to Jafa's method of "worrying the image," which he describes as bending of regulated time. Many of these photographs are double exposures, which is a technical way of showing two different moments simultaneously. In that way, I am also bending time through photography instead of film. Furthermore, just as Jafa considers the hand-cranked camera a more appropriate instrument to create unstable visual frequencies, I think film cameras, like the one I used to create these images, offer a similar opportunity to create the offbeat.

As I reflect upon these photographs, the outliers, they all somehow have a surreal quality. They are not overly focused, have light leaks, are cut and bent, people are absent, and the composition is slightly off-center. To some, these qualities may qualify them as mistakes and undesirable, and maybe the offbeat to many would also be considered a mistake. However, I think by choosing these photographs and choosing the unstable, I am undoing regulation and expanding these images' impact instead of discarding them as errors.

I also really resonated with Jafa's use of the archive, and I was thinking about how these photographs represent my archive. I think the archive connects so well to James Snead's "cut" and "repetition." The archive is constantly open to cuts. Every time one revisits it, one might see something new and connect different pieces to one another. The original intent of these photographs is completely different from what I am doing with them today, and that is what's so special about the archive and the idea of the cut.
