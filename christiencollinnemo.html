Christien Ayers, Collin Riggins, Nemo Newman 

Our piece this week, in response to Kahlil Joseph’s Until the Quiet Comes (2013) and Wildcat (2013) and in conversation with Arthur Jafa’s Apex (2013), is an experiment in sonic and temporal manipulation utilizing footage from our third composition. We open in silence, creating space for the viewer to form their own opinion about the footage presented. We then disrupt the viewers expectation by inserting a jarring still from Apex. At this point, peaceful music plays to confuse the shocking still. We continue this pattern of creating comfort, followed by confusion, by changing the sonic and temporal elements to regulate the viewer’s emotions and expectations. We continue to insert Apex stills at pivotal moments to start to form the narrative of the piece. On the level of narrative, our video is meant to offer a perspective on Black folks seeking rest in America. Each time that we come close to peace and rest, something massively upending occurs and disrupts the peace. Further, even after the events pass, it is impossible to forget Black-facing horrors; it becomes even more difficult to find peace. Ultimately, the video ends with a child (Nemo’s little brother, Jaeden) singing the song Enemy. Enemy tells a narrative of people perceiving the singer as an enemy and thus making them one. We chose to end with this clip because it captures the emotional resistance and acceptance that Black folks have to find in order to achieve even a semblance of peace in this world. We must ultimately come to terms with the travesties that we will experience, or we will be overwhelmed when the next tragedy occurs.


The Tools of the Master is our engagement with John Akomfrah’s work on digitopia and Kara Keeling’s concepetualization of queer temporality. In terms of the latter, we were struck by the idea that the hypervisibility of black people is a product of white supremacy. As a result, we asked how we can express something so beautiful to us—the expression of black joy—while also protecting the subjects of our photos. As a result, we took one shot and duplicated it numerous times, altering the opacity and their placement to create a work where black joy the people depicted are perceptible, though maybe not entirely recognizable. In terms of digitopia, we then asked how we could capture the limits of analog materiality and offer a way forward—express a yearning for a digital, afrofuturistic utopia. For each photo, we altered the curves along the axis of either the image’s reds, greens, or blues. As a result, we achieved a visual representation of the limits of film, which forced black skin to appear in film stocks that were not made for black skin. Additionally, under the amalgamation of these altered photos, we underlaid a negative of the original photo. Its phantom-like presence speaks to the indelible impact that film had on the editing process, as discussed by Akomfrah; indeed, the unforgiveness of film made it haunt final productions. Ultimately, we ask how we can use these tools of the master for us: folks who were not factored into these tools’ creation. As audio plays behind the print of this digital, quasi-analog piece, an ominous liberatory potential emanates from our work. Bringing together and manipulating a range of sounds, the sonic mirrors the value of techno culture in Detroit; it serves as a beacon of black ingenuity during massive change. It contends that we can, in fact, make whatever medium ours.


For this week’s collaboration we bring Saidiya Hartman’s “Venus in Two Acts” and Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments into conversation with The Sweet Flypaper of Life.

We were particularly captivated by Hartman’s attempt to tell the story of women only known through the violence sin the violence in order to capture a new, possibly liberating memory of them. In a sense, Hartman redirects our gaze. Yet, as Hartman finds, it is impossible to entirely escape violence, which ultimately is germaine to many of these stories. We found this to be the crux of The Sweet Flypaper of Life, Hughes and DeCarava’s guided the reader to view Harlemites through other people, their environment, and their feelings. These stylistic techniques prompt the viewer to understand historical black figures not from how they are conventionally portrayed (which is white washed and dismissive of their humanity), but to view their experiences with them. Through them. So, in order to engage with these ideas, we created four classical portraits of women, but redacted the violence. In turn, what we ended up making was four blank greyscale drawings, because the idea of a classical female portrait is inherently violent; classical portraiture of female subjects subjects them to the Male Gaze and reduces them to an object for ogling. The only way that we were able to truly capture the subject without any semblance of violence was to forego capturing them in any recognizable form. We then hung the images in balance with each other as an homage to Hartman’s quote that suggests our lives and the lives of these women will always hang in the balance. We cast Hartman quotes on the floor below the hanging portraits in order to capture the idea of lost lives during the voyage, but also to enforce the idea of dependency on written archival narratives to imagine untold people of the past. The final element we included was the use of an essential oil titled “African Queen” to create an olfactory frequency. We were keen on using this specific essential oil because it is an idealized imagination of an African Queen, which all of these portraits were meant to represent.

In his work, Arthur Jafa explores motion and the absence thereof. Each film is a series of images, or a series of moving images. Jafa uses this dichotomous physical property–motion–to poke and prod the concept of Blackness. In Love is the message in particular, Jafa sutures a series of found footage together, creating a seamless flow; the movement of one individual seems to map perfectly onto a following clip of another person moving in synchronous motion, often in a different environment under drastically different context. Yet, in between some of these clips are images of celestial objects. Our work aims to engage with these motifs. We ask how does a star, burning in a vacuum in its raptorious silence, interact with our understanding of Blackness? Does it flow or depart?

We reckon that these celestial bodies are situated between images of Black people moving with a similar grace because they simply are; these celestial bodies offer another understanding of Blackness that refuses to be tethered to pain, defamation, and all the other taxation that comes with an earthly existence. The act of existing in a vacuum with all of their effervescent beauty offers another valid understanding of Blackness—something that Jafa proves takes many forms and has no bounds.

In our film, we intimately engage with this concept. We traveled to a site that is mired in violent, bloody history: the Princeton Battleground State Park. Here, American revolutionaries fought British troops to secure a liberty that would paradoxically result in even more oppressive bondage for Black people. Yet, although Nemo is shown existing in, and therefore embodying, this violent space, we sought to capture them beyond. Like a celestial object, the act of being is all we ask of Nemo. In that sense, they come to practice a sort of Black celestial embodiment. Furthermore, the juxtaposition of flight, expressed through found footage of birds, adds to our exploration of Black celestial embodiment. As the incendiary soundtrack, Walkin by Denzel Curry plays, we ask: What does it mean for Black people to have the audacity to continue walkin? To persist through age-old violence? What does it mean to imagine flight? In Dreams are Colder than Death, Fred Moten discusses Black people in the hull of the ship. There, he argues, fantasies of flight generated a radical, disruptive, imaginative capacity. In an iteration of that hull, we aim to do the same.

We engage with Jafa further as we manipulate the progression of our clips, cutting out portions of a sequence, altering the speed at which it plays, and using repetition. These techniques aim to demand something of the viewer; we oftentimes disrupt their expectations of how Nemo ought to move; we cut the audio, disrupting expectations further, forcing the audience to sit with Nemo in silence. In short, the audience must let Blackness be, in the chaotic, graceful obscurity of a celestial body, demanding nothing more.


Our first radical composition is in conversation with the materials covered in weeks one and two. We pay particular attention to the visual frequencies of Carrie Mae Weems’ People of a Darker Hue, Richard Wright’s 12 Million Black Voices, and the Seegers’ Afro-American Worksongs in a Texas State Prison.

First, our work examines the relationship between the Black individual and the Black collective which is an underlying theme in all three selected works. In Worksongs, this is captured in the transition of frames—a group of men are depicted laboring in idyllic unison only to be cut by a close up shot that fills the screen with one man’s body. More interestingly, the antiquated film equipment furthers the connection between the individual laborer and the group. In shots of the entire group, the viewer hears a sequence of call and response; a leader cries out a set of lyrics to his comrades which they meet with a concerted, collective lick of their own. Yet, despite these two distinct actors being sonically decipherable, the fuzzy nature of the film blurs this distinction. We cannot quite make out who is the individual leader and who is the collective. A tension arises in this exchange, as a seamless flow between the Black individual and the Black collective is achieved. Our work wrestles with this relationship, as we separate a physically bound collection of Black faces and manipulate how they exist in relation to each other. In our film, depictions of Black individuals sometimes stand alone, followed by a succession of more individuals. Other times, these Black faces are conjoined together in one big shape reminiscent of Africa—a formation which serves as the canvas for our drip drawing. Yet, no matter which form these 12 million Black faces appear, there is an unyielding thread that stitches them together. So, as the individual pieces of our drip drawing are passed around in class, there is no question as to what collective they spawn from. The flow between the individual and the Black diaspora is seamless.

Another element of our composition translates the abstraction of time in the Black experience. This is demonstrated by the visual frequencies of our selected materials. For example, through prose, Richard Wright conveys time cyclically. The rhythm of time in his fast-paced telling of Black movement throughout history shows Black people facing oppression by the “Lords of the Land.” He expresses a deep, low tone. Eventually, through Black persistence, we feel a sense of triumph as Black people migrate to urban environments and experience new opportunities. The tone lightens as we revel in what feels like a high. Yet, this is disrupted as cotton is replaced by kitchenettes, the Lords of Land substituted by the Bosses of Buildings. Again, we hear a tonality that vibrates at a similar frequency to the story’s beginning. The notion of time’s linearity is even challenged by Wright’s separation of southern and northern Black communities. As he audaciously traces each groups development throughout history, they seem to exist on two different temporal planes: one caught in a state of stagnation, the other ostensibly (and maybe nominally) progressing forward. Our work translates this rhythm through our dealing of sound. Towards the end of the audio portion of our radical composition, the voices of gospel singers oscillate between clarity and obscurity; the pitch of their voices go in and out of tune; throughout, samples of song and film created by Black artists drone on in a dissonant trance. All of this echoes the continual march upward of Black people, thwarted and sent falling downward throughout their history—from slavery to emancipation, from emancipation to Jim Crow and so on. Moreover, we abstract time quite explicitly by the idiosyncratic progression and reversal of time exhibited by timestamps that adorn the upper right corner of each frame.

In short, our first radical composition encapsulates the visual frequency of abstraction found across the work we have explored thus far. We ask the audience to ponder, how does the Black individual navigate in relation to their kin? Their ancestry? How does Blackness abide by time? Or does it refuse to abide at all?

Last week, we looked at a series of portraits and landscapes shot by photographers as part of the Farmers Security Administration’s efforts. This archive of photos was accompanied by text written by Richard Wright. Although there were similarities in form between last week’s 12 Million Black Voices and this week’s The Sweet Flypaper of Life, we realized that our gaze was prompted to do something different when interacting with each work. Through Langston Hughes’ prose, each photograph welcomes our gaze and directs it through lenses of family and the 1950s Harlem environment to achieve an intimate understanding of each subject. So, while Richard Wright challenges the indexicality of photographs by placing them in conversation with Black history (which is to say American history), Hughes directs us to understand the immediate social totality of the Black Harlemite. This is achieved by Sister Mary Bradley’s fictional narration that reflects on her family. For example, we are introduced to Rodney who at first glance may come across as a really shallow figure. At least, that is the message that is conveyed when Sister Mary recounts his parents “washing their hands of him” and dismissing him from their lives. Yet, Sister Mary never lets him fade from her narration. Rather, he is constantly invoked. Throughout Hughes’ writing, we begin to understand him in relation to his family. His sister Melinda and her husband Jerry serve as foils to Rodney. We understand Rodney through the poignant description of his son. In short, our gaze is guided to understand Rodney through the diverse array of people living in Harlem. And in turn, as our gaze probes the series of photographs that accompany this text, depicting different men in Harlem, we begin to see reflections of Rodney buried within them. Furthermore, just how Hughes makes us understand Harlemites in relation to other people, he also makes us understand them in relation to their environment. On pages 22 and 23, a spread of very distinct landscapes are displayed. Hughes succinctly describes how Rodney uniquely interacts with each environment, or sometimes even possibly fails to. By engaging with photos of these spaces, we are able to piece together the deep social totality of Rodney. We effectively begin to understand space through Rodney, and Rodney through space. This careful guiding of the viewer’s gaze is what we ultimately hoped to achieve in our composition.

Working within an archive

Similar to how Langston Hughes works within Roy DeCarava’s archive of photos, for our composition, we worked within an existing archive of photographs that capture a very local Black community at Princeton taken by Collin.

Guiding the gaze

Using twine and empty space, we guide the viewer’s gaze to land upon a final moment that is sweet, tender, and very much a symbol of communal love. To reach this image, one must first engage (and wrestle) with a series of environments and individuals.

Dissonance

A key aspect of The Sweet Flypaper of Life was the dissonance that exists between generations. When Sister Mary Bradley refuses the invitation from the messenger, she looks outward onto the Harlem community and attempts to make sense of the younger generations who fill the streets. There is naturally a sense of distance. She is not always able to fully understand each individual. This is characterized through the juxtaposition of jazz and blues. Sister Mary wonders why jazz is the only thing that makes Rodney move fast. As we learned in Harlem Is Nowhere, we know that older generations found jazz to lack the profound humanity that was captured by the blues. Yet, even despite Sister Mary’s inability to fully grasp young people in Harlem’s affinity to jazz, she is still able to retain a sense of understanding—a hapticity to the subjects pictured on the page. This is evidenced in one moment where she notes “when i was young, they used to play “hot”, but now they play “cool”. But they get just as excited as I did.” So, even while there is dissonance in the rhythms of each generation—the older narrator, and the younger subjects—they retain an intimate hapticity with each other nonetheless. We attempted to capture this in our work by displaying subjects, scenes, and colors that may appear dissonant at first glance. The bold yellow of the second panel may starkly contrast the soft violets in the fourth panel. Yet, by wrestling through these ostensible differences and probing the connections that span between subjects and environments, the viewer, too, is able to achieve a haptic relationship across difference and realize the beauty in doing so.
