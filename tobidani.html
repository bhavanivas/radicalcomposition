Our radical composition deals with embodiment, which we explore through three facets of our understanding of the term. The first deals with representations of embodiment, the second, with the space that necessitates an embodied experience, and the last, the physicality of embodiment which includes both psychological and material space.

Representations of Embodiment

The primary part of our composition investigated representations of embodiment through the photo/text relationship. We identified three modalities of representation: CORROBORATION, CONTRAST, and PUSH/PULL. Each of these modalities illustrated the ways in which we saw the image relating to the text in The Sweet Flypaper of Life, Twelve Million Black Voices, and Harlem Is Nowhere. CORROBORATION is when the image and text seem in semantic alignment with one another. In the case of CONTRAST, the image and text are at odds with each other, and PUSH/PULL is a third bucket in which there is a less didactic or discernible relationship. PUSH/PULL illuminates how the proximity of text and image can enrich our readings of both parts. The text might allow us to reach clarity about an aspect of the image that was otherwise nebulous, or might complicate what might otherwise be an easily digestible image. The text is able to worry the image, to complicate our reading, and vice versa.

The purpose of our handouts and our exercise in class was to attempt to arrive at additional photo/text modalities. Our hope is to continue to build on these throughout the semester.

—Tobi

These photo/text relationships can be further explored through the process of collage, where multiple relationships of corroboration, contrast, or push/pull can be put in conversation with one another on the same canvas. This collage used quotes from Twelve Million Black Voices, alongside images from the same work, in the lower part of the piece, and images from The Sweet Flypaper of Life in the upper. When the Twelve Million Black Voices images and text are put in conversation with each other, the frequencies resonate in corroboration with one another. In contrast, using the more optimistic and individualized images from The Sweet Flypaper of Life alongside Wright’s description of Harlem creates a sort of dissonance between the two. These photo/text relationships create different representations of embodiment for a viewer, with different frequencies and relationships between frequencies leading to different interpretations of the texts/images.

— Dani



Placing these words and images in the space of a map puts these relationships in a physical context. Twelve Million Black Voices revolves around the experience of the Great Migration along the Ohio River and the differences between the lived realities and struggles of blacks in the Southern versus Northern lands. The 1900 Census map that serves as the background for this work shows the percentage black population per country and the blue line in the middle of the midwest is the Ohio River. The usage of the physical settings of the works conveys a sense of embodiment in the understanding that these photos and text allude to real lived experiences of the people they chronicle over a physical space. David’s work deals with this idea of embodiment as a physical and psychological experience of an individual.

Our composition this week was inspired by our reflections on Arthur Jafa’s process. We began our conversation by talking about the difficulty of these images, how it felt to watch these videos and to be confronted with the content. We then thought about the labor that Jafa would have had to endure in comparison to what we felt the labor of viewing was that we were tasked with. While we were confronted with these quick pulses of images, in the construction of the video, Jafa would have had to sit with these images, take them in slowly and at length.

We began to think about how our radical composition could reflect, in some way, the labor of Jafa’s creation. This was a real shift for me, and Dani in that we were beginning to think about radical composition as a process and not a product. We chose these 10 images each that were most compelling to us. Per Jafa’s quote, it was our way of locating ourselves in the work.

We then each made these compositions in which we varied the scale and positions of each image in relation to the connections we saw and the intensity of the image’s visual frequency. From this, we were able to have two image compositions that were reflective of ourselves. We then brought the two of us together through finding these relationships across our chosen images. This act of making relationships and bringing ourselves through our compositions together was our act of radical composition, directly paralleling Jafa’s process of radical composition.

We were inspired by the title of the video apex and the perceived contrast between the title which suggested the idea of these images reaching a crescendo of visual frequency when in reality the violence and intensity of the images felt constant throughout the video. In some moments we felt as if the video was leading up to a scream without the sound ever being uttered. The music behind the piece was made directly after our conversation about this “scream” and this apex, when I found myself thinking about the topics while improvising. I feel that religion and music and art are sort of ways to scream out frustration in a productive way, which I tried to convey.

We then took the images and the connections that we had found between our own interpretations and in our video work, tried to connect the visual frequency of these images and connections with the tones and frequencies of the music. The hope was that the build up into this release of the gospel choir would feel more like an apex in our video than it did in Jafa’s. Made as a screen recording of a slideshow, the method of manually changing the slides in the making of the film also invokes something similar to Black Visual Intonation in its irregularity.




My thesis focuses on a type of dataset framing called sonification. Sonification is the conceptualization of data through sound. There are many different algorithms that can be used to sonify, each a different way of mapping notes into sound, usually with higher numbers corresponding to higher notes. Sonification first started gaining traction when entities such as NASA began using it to help people understand the layout of galaxies. People have since used the technique to make better sense of income inequality on NYC subway lines and to convey spatial data to the visually impaired, as well as to people who are less scientifically literate. Various projects have also worked on sonifying climate data in particular, showing increasing temperatures and carbon dioxide levels over time through a more impactful medium.

The sounds used in this project illustrate downscaled temperature projections for the Representative Construction Pathway 4.5, which is one of the many climate models that attempt to predict future atmospheric actions based off of past, determined by certain tipping points in human action.

Because sound impacts a more limbic part of the brain, it is often more memorable than visual stimulus. Different types of music, as well as different moments within a given piece of music, correspond to different types of emotional appeals to an audience. These differences have the potential to make a listener feel different ways about the data at hand. Some of them could cause the data to be stored as a more potent memory than others, having a greater impact on future actions. Data doesn’t have to be numerical. It often instead consists of personal stories that make up a cultural evolution. Music has the ability to condense these stories into a narrative.

—Dani


Dani’s sounds capture the history of a potential future. It is a speculative sound, one that resolves the possibilities of tomorrow into the sonic experiences of today. Sound is a medium – as Dani shows through her research – that allows one to travel through and outside of space and time delineations and linearities. In John Akomfrah’s The Last Angel of History, the data thief surfs across time and space to explore the artifacts of Black sonic culture. This video explores notions of afrofuturism, and how sound can cover the distance between the current and the new, as does Dani’s research. In the film, Greg Tate compares Black existence to science fiction through similarities such as cultural dislocation and alienation. This dislocation is both spatial, if we consider the Transatlantic Slave Trade, and temporal, for the consequential loss of generational wisdom and the sustained efforts to thwart the Black population. Khalil Joseph’s Wildcat provides an inlet into a lesser known facet of Black history. His video shows images of Black people in what might appear to be a hostile landscape that recalls the material geography of slavery. And yet Blackness is sublime here, it is awesome and perhaps frightful but culturally deep.

I would like to affirm the correlation between the Western, as seen through Joseph’s film Wildcat, and science fiction, as seen through Last Angel of History. One genre recalls the past, one references the future. The idea of the ‘new frontier’, or ‘far out’. These notions coalesce in the character of a space cowboy. The essence of the Black diaspora necessitates a collapse of time and space; this becomes possible through the character of the space cowboy.

Our video is a speculative biography of the space cowboy. It layers Dani’s sonification under our chosen images, riffing off of our past composition based on Arthur Jafa’s Apex. The character of the space cowboy, in combination with the climate change music reflects the maintenance of autonomies that one has over their potential futures. We are able to act now, in the present, learning from the past, in order to create a more favorable future. The afrofuturist space cowboy traverses through multiple lineages and grants us the gift of futurity.

—Tobi

“The intention here isn’t anything as miraculous as recovering the lives of the enslaved or redeeming the dead, but rather laboring to paint as full a picture of the lives of the captives as possible. This double gesture can be described as straining against the limits of the archive to write a cultural history of the captive, and, at the same time, enacting the impossibility of representing the lives of the captives precisely through the process of narration.”

Pg. 11, "Venus in Two Acts," Saidiya Hartman


In "Venus in Two Acts,"" Hartman presents the absurdities of attempting to construct a fuller picture of the lives of enslaved Black women with the extant archive. Hartman describes the violence of the archive, and how Black women exist in limited representations that only position them in relation to the violence of slavery. The text describes how these women exist in traces of death and subjugation that impose invisibility on their persons.

Hartman ponders the risk of attempting to redress this archival violence; she discusses the negotiation between attempting to construct an emancipatory image through narrative, and the potential risk of this narrative harmfully romanticizing the truth of the violence. In the end, Hartman proposes her method of critical fabulation to generate stories from the archive that give space to the fullness of Black women’s lives.

Hartman’s method takes form through the subjunctive. In my printmaking works, I have also explored analogous methods of generating from an absence, of using negative space – the absence of information, of history, of trace – to construct an image. This printmaking technique is called the reductive monoprint. It is a method that allows you to draw through erasure. To allow an image to materialize through and despite continual erasures. In this week’s radical composition, we confronted Hartman’s ask of us to imagine these two women in the hold of the ship. We produced a series of prints that explore the impossibility and yet the necessity of this task.

—Tobi

As someone who had only made prints once before briefly, I was struck by the special type of repetition that the process requires, especially Tobi’s technique of generating from an absence. This process demands that you be concentrated in the present moment as you ink each plate, form each image, transfer each print. It is easy to see how somebody could enter into a sort of flow through this process, being focused on each action and yet somewhat dissociated.

In the past, we’ve discussed the nature of repetition, how this repetition induces different types of trance, and how intentional repetition can lead to rhythmic actions, therefore making the everyday more beautiful and conscious. The rhythm of printmaking, specifically, can generate a better understanding of the narratives and images at hand as they are defamiliarized into the lines and shades of artwork and yet simultaneously familiarized through repetition.

There is a lack of complete control over the final product in printmaking, and the same image can never be generated twice. These things have striking parallels with the creation of a narrative from an archive, where the same historical information becomes different things as it is expanded upon, interpreted, and abstracted in different contexts.

—Dani

Trance can be defined as a state of abstraction. All of the prints that Dani and I made operate in this state of abstraction. Particularly, the portraits modeled off of the illustrations in Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments. These portraits reference images of women who remain largely unknown, apart from their relationship to the text that Hartman fabulates. The ephemeral and gestural quality to the prints reflect this abstract form of representation. They calcify the idea that these women can never really be known.

In Cameron Rowland’s work, he appropriates found objects. By placing objects already in existence in the context of his practice, accreting objects around themes of slavery, property, and policing, the objects take on a new meaning; the archive is complicated. Semantics shift when the Institute of Contemporary Arts’ front door is reframed as an art object that symbolizes the material remains of slavery and the continued dehumanization of Caribbean bodies. We wanted to use another printmaking technique to reimagine the found object. This technique is called a trace monoprint and we used it to create abstracted reflections of the opening quotes in Rowland’s exhibition statement. How do these words change, lose, or gain meaning through the rewriting and mirroring of their forms?

—Toni

A trace monoprint transfers a positive, and yet reflected, image from the plate. We used this to write quotes from Rowland’s texts, reflecting and transforming the historical words into a state that seems just barely illegible. This sort of process is related to Hartman’s writings on the archive. Words that meant one thing when they were first recorded are often consciously and unconsciously twisted and changed as they are compounded with other narratives over time.

Moreover, the trance state induced by this process meant that the words changed in my personal understanding of their meaning, as well. On one hand, the larger meaning of the text somewhat disappears as one focuses on writing each word over and over. However, specific words gain more meaning when they are honed in on. For example, I found myself thinking more deeply about the fact that the Fugitive Slave Clause uses the word “escape” as I was writing it out. This word connotes the immorality of the slave owners. This is notable because it is used in a legislative context with the purpose of making that immorality legally okay.

—Dani

Conclusion

This week was a sustained reflection on the process of making, and how alternative processes hold different meanings. Hartman and Rowland each have their own methods for complicating, exposing, and resisting the archive. Through this radical composition, Dani and I were able to explore the methods for this that are possible within the medium of printmaking. It is critical that we continue to exhaust the archive, it is our belief that it should not exist as a static artifact, but must always be poked and probed in the hopes of revealing something remarkable.
